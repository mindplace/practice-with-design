Reading Mandeville in the Early Modern Period

Sometime around 1350, an anonymous person writing as one Sir John Mandeville penned a little English book of wonders. This Mandeville was chiefly concerned with conveying his supposed Travels to Jerusalem and then onwards through Asia, incorporating a wide variety of wonders which he bade the reader to believe were meant to be taken as truthful--monstrous races, improbable lands, bizarre tales. This little book, far from falling into obscurity and disuse, saw an explosion of interest all the way through the 17th century at which point it was finally, with some pain, put to rest as not being a factual travel account. Nevertheless, it was extremely popular throughout this long period, and particularly during the 16th and 17th centuries.

These two centuries saw an explosion in the interest in and publication of the literature of travel, to the point that this genre's publication rate and readership was exceeded only by texts dealing with religious issues (1). The Travels enjoyed a brilliant popularity throughout this period and, categorized as travel literature, was printed more often than any non-religious text. At new editions averaging every eight years during the late 16th to 17th centuries, Mandeville was more popular than Chaucer (2).

Certainly the popularity of Mandeville's Travels hinged in part on the fact that it contained several different genre elements that were at the time particularly relevant. There was a pervasive interest in monstrous races, which was the 'essential Mandeville as far as the Elizabethan reader or audience was concerned'--indeed, Mandeville's name was often cited in conjunction with monstrous races such as the Anthropophagi, and some parts of the Travels were printed or circulated that preserved merely the sections relating to such fantastic races (3). And yet the book often appeared as a cited source in such prestigious and important texts as Samuel Purchas' Purchas His Pilgrims (1625).

This growing cultural desire for travel narratives and tales of distant lands, augmented by colonialist expansion and conquest, meant a steady readership even as more modern travel narratives were published. Mandeville persisted even amidst such company as Sir Francis Drake and Martin Frobisher, perhaps because he remained, as Rebecca Ann Bach calls him, the 'ur-traveler' for the contemporary reading public, and his book 'a model for exploration literature' (4).

Regardless of other publications, Mandeville's Travels was 'England's popular baseline of spatial and human geography until the late sixteenth century, and, although its primacy waned rapidly thereafter, its residual influence persisted for at least another century' (5).

The Travels' uncertain position as a factual text

Early scholarship suggested that Mandeville's factual credibility started facing a barrage of attacks, which eventually resulted in the reading public setting him aside. (For example, Richard Hakluyt's Principall Navigations excludes Mandeville from its second edition while including it in the first (1589).) Some held opinions like Sir Walter Raleigh's, who wrote that Mandeville's text had been 'held for fables many yeares' (6). Similarly, while Samuel Purchas included Mandeville in his Pilgrims, Purchas did so with the disclaimer that Mandeville was a ' 'famous Traveller...whose Geographie Ortelius commendeth, howesoever he acknowledgheth his Worke stuffed with Fables' ' (7).

Still, the book did not fall to the realists for a good long time, thanks to the efforts of many outspoken defenders. One recurring response to the Travels argued that modern travelers seemed to be confirming, rather than refuting, the information Mandeville presented. One author mentions in passing that Mandeville's 'Relations deserve more credit than formerly they have had' (8). Mandeville, writes another, 'measur'd of this earthly ball / A greater part then any of his time' for the good of his country, yet

  his Readers threw
Contempt upon his news, more strange than true
Thought his reports, accounting them such toyes
And sigments as phantastiques oft devise.
Yet afterwards when travellers did make
Further discov'ries, and surveyes did take
Of this main Globe, they found his wonders true
In th' greater part, and gave him praises due
To his high merits, making him thereby
A just amends for wrongful obloquy. (9)

New discoveries 'found his wonders true / In th' greater part' rather than discounted his contribution to travel literature. As for others, some texts written in part for the benefit of actual travelers, such as Richard Wiles' History of Trauayle (1577) and Robert Johnson's 'sober' Traveller's Breviat (circa 1600) incorporate Mandeville 'respectfully' as an authoritative traveler and perhaps even a reliable author (10). Clearly there was a well-established tradition of reading Mandeville as partially fantastic but mainly realistic, in that he wrote 'true Travels'. In some way, the question of credibility was not contained simply in Mandeville's book itself so much as the entire genre of travel writing: 'there [was] a good deal of moral unease, not so much about travel writing and fiction, but about how the credulous might regard not just Mandeville but travel accounts and fiction in general' (11).

Despite, or perhaps because of, issues relating to the factual nature of Mandeville's Travels, authors and their literary texts often borrowed from or commented on Mandeville. Raleigh, for example, cites Mandeville in two texts almost twenty years apart, suggesting an 'ambiguous attitude towards what was universally acknowledged at the same time as an unreliable source', an attitude that is reflective of the place Mandeville held in this period: ambiguous and at least partially credible (12).

Why did Mandeville remain so popular? Because in this period where people were starting to experience the distant and the exotic, Mandeville continued to deliver wonders.

Thinking about the factuality and credibility of Mandevillian lore does not seem to really answer why his book was so popular. A better answer would note the reading public's voracious interest in all things exotic and distant. William Wood, publishing in 1634 his account of New England, explains that he had written his text to 'enrich the knowledge of the mind-travelling Reader, or benefit the future Voyager':

Wood's separation of the "mind-travelling Reader" from the "future Voyager" is significant in that it reveals two quite discrete categories of readers with an interest in travel literature, suggesting that the publication of such texts not only disseminated pragmatic information, but also catered to the desires of readers with little or no prospect of actually voyaging, but who nevertheless enjoy viewing exotic shores in their minds' eyes. (13)

David McInnes here argues that travel literature was not merely pragmatic, but fulfilled the desires of 'armchair travelers' for 'exotic shores' as just one of the new types of desirable products that began to become available at in England during this time. Mandeville's Travels, as a symbol of the exotic and the distant, was slowly re-envisioned as an archetype of such products on the market.

Mandeville on the map

Interest and curiosity about travel and fantastic sights abroad was caught up with Mandevillian lore and expressed in literature and drama, and often could be read as commenting on contemporary ideas relating to travel and the desire for the exotic. One literary text that combines Mandeville with factual discussions of England and places abroad is William Warner's second edition of Albion's England, originally published in 1586 and reprinted with additions in 1596. It offered a history of England to the current date and 'was apparently established as a major work before being almost totally forgotten' (14). Of Mandeville, Warner writes, 'VVho reads Sir Iohn de Mandeuil his Trauels, and his Sights, / That wonders not? and wonder may, if all be true he wrights' and adds that 'most [readers], now, modernly approu'd' of Mandeville as a truthful traveler and narrator.

In the second edition, starting from Book 11 onwards, Warner re-envisions Mandeville as a knight in love with an English lady named Elenor. Warner partially recasts Mandeville's Travels in the letters his Mandeville writes to Elenor, for example comparing her with the Amazons he encounters in the Travels: 'The Amazones, those lustie Girles, beleeue me lik't me Well: / But nothing in the best of them but doth in you excell' (15). Warner ventriloquizes Mandeville, yet places him amidst earnestly written histories. His recasting of Mandeville's Travels makes Mandeville into 'the archetypical traveler-figure, ideal patron for a discussion of England's maritime exploits' which Warner spends some time recounting (16). There is a sense here of a kind of nationalistic and prideful propaganda, which Mandeville's presence supports as a definitively English and grandiose traveler. Warner's use of Mandeville reflects the partially-credible reception the Travels enjoyed--as a useful and entertaining participant among more credible travel accounts.

The Mandevillian traveller in Othello

Mandeville shows up in Shakespeare's Othello, in Othello's speech explaining how it was that Desdemona had fallen in love with him. Both she and Brabantio, Othello says, 'questioned me the story of my life', which he obligingly related to them both. Othello has quite a 'traveller's history', signaling, with this phrase, a very Mandevillian kind of past, just as he mentions elements commonly attributed to Mandeville (17). He has seen, Othello claims,

  the cannibals that each other eat,
The Anthropophagi, and men whose heads
Do grow beneath their shoulders. These things to hear
Would Desdemona seriously incline, [...]
  and with a greedy ear
Devour up my discourse... (18)

It is merely the relation of his story, Othello says, that makes Desdemona fall in love with him, and at the end of his speech, having made it in answer to Brabantio's claim that Othello had bewitched his daughter, says that 'This is the only witchcraft I have used' (19). Mandevillian lore, here, is at the heart of the 'witchcraft' that enthralls Desdemona. The cultural commentary here is on those people who, like Desdemona, find themselves enthralled with the monsters and 'odd discoveries' of Mandevillian lore and travel narratives in general. They 'devour' such tales and are bewitched by them. Othello represents the theatre itself as a place where such tales and creatures are played, for the enticement and enthralled interest of audience-as-Desdemona. He is the 'alien witness', as Ladan Niayesh calls him, in relating to Desdemona the strange people and places he has seen, thus becoming himself a Mandevillian figure (20). He parallels Albion's England in the way his Travels are related to Desdemona explicitly for her entertainment and resulting in her affection. Just as Warner creates a romantic image of a knight relating his tales to his lady, raising her up as he does, so does Shakespeare cast Othello in this heroic position in relation to Desdemona.

And yet, all this pleasure in the exotic and the distant can be addictive, and Mandeville's book was the best example of it.

The literature of travel is personal, fulfilling, and pleasurable; it entertains and fulfills the desire for alterity and the exotic without inconveniencing the would-be traveler with actual travel. Yet it is also addictive; it may beguile to the point of leaving readers lacking the desire to continue living normal lives. Or such is the premise of Richard Brome's The Antipodes, a play in which young Peregrine, an avid reader of Mandeville, is losing his mind to his desire to experience exotic lands and races. Peregrine holds Mandeville in such high respect that he thinks 'Iohn [Mandeville] / Dwels up almost at Paradice' (21). The Doctor, called in to cure Peregrine of his madness, acknowledges that 'Mandevile went farre' and asks what Peregrine thinks of 'Drake, our famous Countriman'; Peregrine answers, 'Drake was a Dy'dapper to Mandevile, / Candish, and Hawkins, Furbisher, all our voyagers / Went short of Mandevile' (22). His mad esteem for Mandeville comments on contemporary culture that similarly held Mandeville as a particularly interesting and important English traveler, regardless of the availability of more contemporary accounts. Brome argues, through Peregrine's madness, that while Mandeville may have some truth to him, his book is problematic because of its enthralling nature, the way it allows people to become addicted to travel and exotic lands. He echoes Ben Jonson's Lord Frampul, who too discusses his Travels in terms of addiction:

  I am he
Have measur'd al the Shires of England over:
Wales, and her Mountains, seen those wilder Nations,
Of People in the Peak, and Lancashire;
Their Pipers, Fidlers, Rushers, Puppet-masters,
Juglers and Gypsies, all the sorts of Canters,
And Colonies of Beggars, Tumblers, Ape carriers;
For to these Savages I was addicted,
To search their Natures, and make odd Discoveries!
And here my Wife, like a she-Mandevile,
Ventred in disquisition after me. (23)

Frampul 'implies that he is constitutionally a Mandeville', styling himself an explorer rather than a puppet-master, and his catalog of peoples are the 'exotic races' of the English countryside which he has explored (24). Even his wife that he calls a 'she-Mandevile', in the way she 'ventred in disquisition' after him, becomes Mandevillian in her following him--just as Peregrine becomes Mandevillian in wishing to emulate his hero.

Brome's Antipodes is based on the question of the addictive quality of the desire for exotic travel. As David McInnis argues, 'The Antipodes is very much about travel, but specifically about the psychology of travel, not the physicality of voyaging' in theorizing 'a common travel concern that had never before received such full attention on stage: a humor that can best be described as a combination of wanderlust and vicarious travel' (25). Peregrine suffers of 'Mandeville-based fantasies' for monstrous races and strange places; it represents the 'moral and social decay [that] was already endemic to Renaissance London' (26): Peregrine's 'addiction' is used as a mirror serving to reflect overly-avid readers of travel literature. Brome shows that desire for exotic travel has 'captured the English imagination'; it is not 'actual travel or the political context in which most early modern travel occurred' that is important, but rather the way people become immersed in such fantastical adventures as Mandeville provides for Peregrine and the reading public at large (27).

Mandeville humanizes the monstrous, and The Tempest concurs: who are we to judge, when the world really may be full of wonders?

Mandevillian tales are more than just addictive. There is a sense of truthfulness somewhere in the fantastic tales presented in travel literature, as Shakespeare's Tempest points out. Antonio, having seen Prospero's island and some of the natives, declaring that he will now believe what travelers claim, pronounces, 'Travellers ne'er did lie, / Though fools at home condemn 'em' (28). Sebastian and Gonzalo agree:

Sebastian: [...] Now will I believe
That there are unicorns, that in Arabia
There is one tree, the phoenix's throne, one phoenix
At this hour reigning there. (29)

The phoenix, described in the Travels, is one of the more easily recognizable mythical elements of the book. Sebastian, in announcing his belief, underscores that he does so in real time: the phoenix, he says, is 'At this hour reigning' in Arabia, in existence at the very moment of his speech. Gonzalo, in turn, contemplates whether he would be believed in Europe if he tried to describe what he had seen:

Gonzalo: If in Naples
I should report this now, would they believe me--
If I should say, I saw such islanders?
For certes these are people of the island,
Who though they are of monstrous shape, yet note,
Their manners are more gentle-kind than of
Our human generation you shall find
Many, nay, almost any. [...]
  When we were boys,
Who would believe that there were mountaineers
Dewlapped like bulls, whose throats had hanging at 'em
Wallets of flesh? Or that there were such men
Whose heads stood in their breasts? (30)

Not only does he acknowledge the monstrous natives he had seen as real, but humanizes them by complimenting their manners, being more 'gentle-kind' than those of normal people, closely echoing the way Mandeville's book often deals matter-of-factly with the monstrous races he encounters. Gonzalo also mentions mountaineers, or people living in mountainous regions, and equates them with 'men / Whose heads [stand] in their breasts', a reference to Mandeville's Blemmye. Gonzalo here suggests that in having grown up and realized the reality of mountaineers, despite his lack of geographic proximity, so should he believe in the Blemmye. He is claiming that travel has made him more credulous in that the world really does hold the sorts of fantastical beings that Mandeville offers.

In his long tenure in the public eye, Mandeville managed to touch a lot of hearts, and leave his mark on many texts.

Mandeville's influence in literature dealing with exotic places reaches far. Mandevillian lore seems present in a variety of texts of this period, whether by being mentioned or discussed directly or simply being an important text for shaping how people thought about travel literature. It touches Marlowe's Tamburlaine, regarding which Richard Hillman argues that contemporary audiences 'may have thought' of Mandeville while watching it, because of mentions such as that of Prester John as a king in Africa (31). Charles Mosley similarly argues that Sir Thomas More may have drawn on Mandeville for Utopia, although it seems more likely that More's text simply brings Mandeville to mind through the close association between his text and the literature of travel in general, in its exploration of English culture shown by contrasting it with a very different kind of place (32). That is, Utopia, and texts such as The Island Princess, may be associated with Mandeville in the way Mandeville shares with such texts a readership base, and in that both Mandeville and the early modern texts straddle ground between social interest in the exotic and early colonialist propaganda. In this way, even as he was still being read in the early modern, Mandeville's Travels slowly began participating in the literature of colonialism, as the question of exotic peoples and places abroad became one of conquest as well as discovery, as highlighted in the connection between his book and works such as Eastward Hoe, The Antipodes, The Island Princess, and others.

Mandeville may have been slowly losing his authority as a 'true traveler', although some writers contended for a while of the rise, rather than the fall, of his credibility. Regardless, his book remained an important text for social issues relating to travel, especially, as this paper has shown, to literature and drama, and remained at the forefront of the literature of travel well into the 17th century.
